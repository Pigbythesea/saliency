## Fit to the core research question

Your stated central question is narrower and more ambitious than ŌĆ£which saliency map looks more human?ŌĆØ It is specifically about whether **human-like adaptive visual attention correlates with modelŌĆōbrain alignment in representational space at both fixation level and fMRI level**. On that criterion, the current project progress only partially adheres to the research agenda. It already addresses part of the behavioral side of the question, and it usefully separates saliency families, but it does **not yet test the cross-level correlation** that makes the project novel. The proposal itself says the key scientific possibility is dissociation: a model may look human-like in saliency while failing in cortical representations, or vice versa. Until the real neural manifest, voxelwise encoding runs, and RSA scores exist, that possibility remains a hypothesis. That means the project should be reframed around a **single cross-level test**: across models, layers, and saliency families, how strongly do behavioral fixation alignment, neural encoding performance, and representational similarity correlate, and where do they dissociate by ROI, task, and compute budget? ## Current state of the field

On the **behavioral saliency** side, the field is more mature, more benchmarked, and more subtle than a simple free-viewing overlap story. The MIT/T├╝bingen benchmark remains the main public reference point, and DeepGaze-family models are still among the most informative baselines to include. DeepGaze IIE was already a strong calibration-focused reference model, and DeepGaze MSDB raised the bar further by explicitly modeling dataset bias and setting new state of the art across MIT300, CAT2000, and COCO-Freeview. The associated ICCV 2025 paper is especially relevant to your project because it shows that inter-dataset transfer drops sharply and that a large share of the gap is attributable to dataset-specific biases rather than a single universal fixation mechanism. That result matters directly for your SALICON/CAT2000/COCO interpretation. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn18search0Ņłéturn18search1Ņłéturn19search1Ņłéturn19search5Ņłü

The same literature also supports your unease with over-interpreting XAI maps as human saliency. Reviews of fixation modeling emphasize that dedicated saliency and scanpath models should be evaluated probabilistically and against strong baselines, including center bias and human upper bounds. K├╝mmerer and BethgeŌĆÖs 2023 review makes this point explicitly. Meanwhile, studies comparing human attention to CNN explanations report that similarity is usually modest, strongly dependent on task and image type, and often higher for manually selected relevance maps than for spontaneous eye movements. That is a major warning for your project design: **free-viewing fixation maps and classifier explanation maps are related, but they are not the same object**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28search2Ņłéturn28search4Ņłéturn27search0Ņłéturn27search1Ņłü

Recent work on **humanŌĆōmachine attention alignment** suggests three non-obvious things. First, better task accuracy does not automatically imply more human-like visual strategy: Fel et al. found a systematic trade-off between ImageNet accuracy and alignment with human visual strategies across many DNNs, then showed that a harmonization procedure could improve both alignment and classification. Second, self-supervised ViTs can develop more human-like gaze structure than standard supervised ViTs; the 2025 Neural Networks paper on DINO-trained ViTs reports distinct head clusters, with a subset of later-layer heads focusing on human-like target regions in video. Third, direct use of human gaze as a training or inference prior is now moving from idea to practice: gaze-informed ViTs and gaze-based token masking both report alignment or efficiency gains in specialized settings. These papers do not solve your problem, but they validate the overall direction. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn1search1Ņłéturn1search9Ņłéturn10view6Ņłéturn10view4Ņłéturn10view5Ņłü

On the **modelŌĆōbrain alignment** side, the field is now driven by platforms and datasets such as Brain-Score, NSD, and Algonauts. Brain-ScoreŌĆÖs central premise is that candidate models should be ranked against suites of neural and behavioral benchmarks, not by engineering accuracy alone. NSD provides the scale that makes contemporary voxelwise encoding and RSA practical: 8 subjects, 7T, thousands of natural scenes, and high-resolution whole-brain measurements. Algonauts made that regime more accessible and directly comparable via a public challenge format. This is exactly the infrastructure your project should plug into, and it is why your markdown note is right to prioritize a real NSD/Algonauts neural run above further behavioral expansion. The deeper lesson from recent brain-alignment work is that architecture alone rarely gives a clean answer. Storrs et al. showed that after fitting and reweighting, diverse DNNs can all predict human IT reasonably well. Conwell et al. then scaled the comparison up and found that CNNs and transformers are surprisingly close in brain predictivity when objective and data are controlled, with CNNs showing only a slight average edge; they also found that instance-level contrastive self-supervision beats non-contrastive self-supervision and can match supervised models, while OpenAI CLIPŌĆÖs strong brain predictivity appears to derive more from data scale/diversity than from language alignment alone in isolation. Finally, there is an important **emerging methodological trend** that should shape your paper design: held-out encoding accuracy alone is no longer considered sufficient for interpretation. ## Novelty and publication potential

The projectŌĆÖs **latent novelty is real**. A paper that jointly measures fixation alignment, fMRI encoding, RSA geometry, and alignment-per-computation across supervised, self-supervised, multimodal, and genuinely adaptive vision models would still be differentiated. The literature has strong papers on individual edges of this square: saliency benchmarking, gaze-aligned training, large-scale brain predictivity, and video-brain alignment. What is still relatively sparse is a controlled study of whether these alignment axes actually travel together, and whether adaptive/selective computation changes that relationship. ## Recommended redesign and roadmap

The immediate redesign should be to **freeze the scientific question and make the benchmark serve it**. The benchmark should no longer be optimized to accumulate more behavioral rows. It should be optimized to answer a single question: which models are aligned behaviorally, neurally, geometrically, and computationally, and do those axes correlate? Your own notes already point in this direction by recommending the first real NSD/Algonauts manifest, one real neural encoding experiment, and RSA before selective-computation or video. The behavioral layer should then be tightened into a **paper-grade control panel** rather than a larger leaderboard. Keep center bias, random, observer controls, and DeepGaze-style reference maps. Keep free-viewing and task-driven datasets separate in analysis and in claims.
